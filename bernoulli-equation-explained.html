Bernoulli Equation

Fundamentals, assumptions, governing equation and engineering applications in fluid systems

What is the Bernoulli Equation?

The Bernoulli equation is one of the most fundamental equations in fluid mechanics. It expresses the conservation of mechanical energy for a flowing fluid by relating pressure energy, kinetic energy and potential energy along a streamline.

Engineers use the Bernoulli equation extensively for analysing pipelines, HVAC duct systems, pumps, fans, flow meters, nozzles and other industrial fluid systems where pressure and velocity continuously change.

Bernoulli Equation

For steady, incompressible and frictionless flow, Bernoulli's equation is written as:

  • P/ρg + V²/2g + z = Constant
  • P = Pressure (Pa)
  • ρ = Fluid density (kg/m³)
  • V = Fluid velocity (m/s)
  • g = Acceleration due to gravity (9.81 m/s²)
  • z = Elevation above reference level (m)

Each term represents energy per unit weight of the fluid. Their sum remains constant along a streamline when no external energy is added or removed.

Meaning of Each Energy Term

  • Pressure Head: Represents energy due to fluid pressure.
  • Velocity Head: Represents kinetic energy associated with fluid motion.
  • Elevation Head: Represents potential energy due to height.

The Bernoulli equation demonstrates that when one form of energy increases, another decreases so that the total mechanical energy remains constant under ideal conditions.

Assumptions of Bernoulli Equation

  • Steady flow
  • Incompressible fluid
  • Flow along a streamline
  • Negligible friction losses
  • No shaft work between sections
  • No heat transfer affecting fluid energy

Practical Bernoulli Equation

Actual engineering systems experience friction losses and often include pumps or turbines. Therefore the practical energy equation becomes:

  • P₁/ρg + V₁²/2g + z₁ + Hp = P₂/ρg + V₂²/2g + z₂ + Hf + Ht
  • Hp = Pump head added
  • Ht = Turbine head extracted
  • Hf = Head loss due to friction and fittings

This form is widely used for hydraulic calculations in industrial piping systems.

Engineering Applications

The Bernoulli equation forms the basis of numerous engineering calculations involving liquid and gas flow.

  • Pipeline pressure analysis
  • Pump system design
  • HVAC duct pressure calculations
  • Fan system analysis
  • Venturi meter flow measurement
  • Orifice and nozzle calculations
  • Water distribution systems
  • Fire protection hydraulics
  • Cooling water systems
  • Industrial process piping
  • Compressed air distribution

Importance in Fluid Engineering

Understanding the relationship between pressure and velocity allows engineers to predict pressure drops, determine pump head requirements, size piping systems and evaluate equipment performance. Bernoulli's principle is also fundamental to the operation of flow measurement devices and many hydraulic machines.

Limitations

  • Not accurate for highly viscous flows without including losses.
  • Compressibility effects become significant at high gas velocities.
  • Shock waves and turbulence require more advanced analysis.
  • Applies only along a streamline unless flow is irrotational.

Engineering Practice

The ideal Bernoulli equation provides an excellent foundation for understanding fluid behaviour. However, real engineering systems always experience friction losses, minor losses and equipment effects. Designers therefore combine Bernoulli's equation with head loss equations, pump performance curves and energy balance methods to obtain accurate system calculations.
