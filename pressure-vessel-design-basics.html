Pressure Vessel Design Basics

Engineering stress concepts used in industrial pressure systems

What is a Pressure Vessel?

A pressure vessel is a container designed to hold gases or liquids at a pressure substantially different from ambient pressure. These systems are widely used in chemical plants, refineries, power plants and process industries.

Because they operate under high internal or external pressure, pressure vessels must be carefully designed to prevent failure due to excessive stress, corrosion, fatigue or thermal loading.

Key Design Stresses

  • Hoop Stress: Circumferential stress acting around the vessel wall.
  • Longitudinal Stress: Stress acting along the length of the vessel.
  • Radial Stress: Usually small compared to hoop and longitudinal stresses.

Basic Formulas

  • Hoop Stress: σh = (P × r) / t
  • Longitudinal Stress: σl = (P × r) / (2t)

Where:

  • P = Internal pressure
  • r = Internal radius
  • t = Wall thickness

Materials Used

  • Carbon steel
  • Stainless steel
  • Alloy steel
  • Composite materials

Safety Considerations

Pressure vessel design includes safety factors to account for pressure surges, corrosion allowance, welding efficiency, and fatigue life. Proper design ensures safe operation under extreme industrial conditions.

Applications

  • Boilers and steam systems
  • Oil & gas separators
  • Chemical reactors
  • Compressed air tanks
  • Heat exchangers and storage vessels

Engineering Notes

Pressure vessel design is based on thin and thick cylinder theory depending on wall thickness ratio. Proper stress distribution, material selection and corrosion protection are essential for long-term reliability and safe operation.

Design standards such as ASME codes are commonly used in industrial applications to ensure structural integrity and compliance.
