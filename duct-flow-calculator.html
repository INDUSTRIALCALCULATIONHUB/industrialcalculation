Engineering Knowledge

What is Duct Flow Rate?

Duct flow rate is the volume of air passing through a duct per unit time. It is a key parameter in HVAC and ventilation system design. It is usually expressed in m³/s, m³/hr, or CFM.

Basic Formula

FormulaDescription
Q = A × VFlow Rate = Area × Velocity
V = Q / AVelocity = Flow Rate / Area
A = Q / VDuct Area = Flow Rate / Velocity

Engineering Notes

  • Higher velocity reduces duct size but increases fan power.
  • Lower velocity reduces noise and pressure loss.
  • Circular ducts have lower friction losses than rectangular ducts.
  • Proper sizing avoids energy wastage in HVAC systems.
  • Always balance airflow, noise, and pressure drop in design.

Calculation Assumptions

  • Steady-state airflow.
  • Uniform air velocity across the duct section.
  • No leakage from the duct system.
  • Air density is assumed constant.
  • Pressure losses are not included.

Duct Pressure Loss Considerations

Duct sizing should consider not only airflow requirements but also pressure losses caused by duct length, bends, dampers, filters, transitions, and fittings. Higher air velocity increases friction losses and fan power requirements. For complete system design, duct pressure drop calculation should be performed along with fan selection.

  • Straight duct friction losses depend on duct size, surface roughness, and velocity.
  • Elbows and fittings create additional dynamic pressure losses.
  • Filters and dampers should be included in total system resistance calculation.
  • Final fan selection requires both airflow and static pressure.

Engineering Best Practices

  • Select duct dimensions based on the required airflow and recommended air velocity.
  • Use circular ducts wherever installation space permits.
  • Minimize the number of elbows and sudden duct transitions.
  • Use smooth-radius bends to reduce pressure loss.
  • Keep rectangular duct aspect ratios as low as practical.
  • Seal duct joints properly to minimize air leakage.
  • Insulate ducts where heat gain, heat loss, or condensation is expected.
  • Balance the duct system during commissioning to achieve the required airflow.
  • Consider future expansion when designing large industrial ventilation systems.
  • Verify final designs using applicable HVAC and industrial ventilation standards.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

1. What is duct flow rate?

Duct flow rate is the volume of air passing through a duct per unit time. It is commonly expressed in m³/s, m³/hr, or CFM and is one of the most important parameters in HVAC and industrial ventilation system design.

2. How is duct flow calculated?

Airflow is calculated using the equation Q = A × V, where Q is the flow rate, A is the duct cross-sectional area, and V is the average air velocity.

3. What is a good air velocity for HVAC ducts?

Typical air velocities are 2–5 m/s for residential HVAC, 5–8 m/s for commercial systems, 8–15 m/s for industrial ventilation, and 15–25 m/s for dust collection systems.

4. Why are circular ducts more efficient than rectangular ducts?

Circular ducts provide lower friction losses, require less fan power, reduce air leakage, and distribute airflow more uniformly than rectangular ducts of the same cross-sectional area.

5. Can this calculator be used for industrial ventilation systems?

Yes. It can be used for preliminary sizing of HVAC ducts, industrial ventilation systems, dust collection ducts, boiler air ducts, fume extraction systems, and similar applications.

6. Does this calculator include pressure loss calculations?

No. This calculator determines airflow, velocity, and duct size only. Pressure loss calculations should be performed separately using duct length, fittings, roughness, and air properties.

7. Which units are supported?

The calculator supports duct dimensions in millimetres and provides results in m³/s, m³/hr, CFM, metres, millimetres, and feet.

8. Can this calculator be used for fan selection?

The calculated airflow can be used as an input for fan selection. However, total static pressure, system resistance, filter losses, and safety margins must also be considered before selecting a fan.
