Heat Transfer

Calculate heat transfer using Fourier's Law of Conduction.

Engineering Knowledge

Heat transfer by conduction is the movement of thermal energy through a solid material due to a temperature difference. According to Fourier's Law of Heat Conduction, the heat transfer rate is directly proportional to the material's thermal conductivity, the heat transfer area, and the temperature difference, while being inversely proportional to the material thickness.

Fourier's Law:

Q = (k × A × ΔT) / L

Symbol Description Unit
Q Heat Transfer Rate W
k Thermal Conductivity W/m·K
A Heat Transfer Area m²
ΔT Temperature Difference °C or K
L Material Thickness m

Effect of Each Parameter

  • Higher thermal conductivity (k): Increases heat transfer.
  • Larger surface area (A): Allows more heat to flow.
  • Higher temperature difference (ΔT): Produces greater heat transfer.
  • Greater insulation thickness (L): Reduces heat transfer.

Typical Thermal Conductivity Values

Material Thermal Conductivity (W/m·K)
Copper 385–400
Aluminium 205
Carbon Steel 45–60
Stainless Steel 14–17
Concrete 1.0–1.8
Glass Wool 0.032–0.040
Mineral Wool 0.035–0.045
Calcium Silicate 0.050–0.070
Polyurethane Foam 0.022–0.028

Engineering Applications

  • Pipe insulation design
  • Steam line heat loss calculations
  • Boiler and furnace insulation
  • Heat exchanger design
  • HVAC duct insulation
  • Building thermal analysis
  • Industrial equipment insulation
  • Cold storage and refrigeration systems

Engineering Notes

  • The formula applies to steady-state one-dimensional heat conduction.
  • Thermal conductivity generally changes with temperature.
  • The calculated heat transfer rate is directly proportional to the exposed surface area.
  • Increasing insulation thickness is one of the most effective methods of reducing heat loss.
  • For multilayer insulation systems, the thermal resistance of each layer should be considered separately.
