Engineering Knowledge

Silo and bunker sizing is an important part of bulk material handling system design. Storage equipment is used in industries such as cement, power generation, steel, mining, fertilizer, food processing and chemical plants to store and discharge large quantities of bulk solids safely and efficiently.

The required storage capacity depends on material properties, plant production rate, storage duration, equipment arrangement and flow characteristics. Engineers normally consider both the geometric volume and the effective usable volume because bulk materials do not occupy the complete internal volume due to the formation of material slopes and hopper discharge requirements.

What is a Silo?

A silo is a vertical storage structure generally used for storing fine or granular bulk materials such as cement, fly ash, grain, limestone powder and other industrial powders. Most silos are circular because the cylindrical shape provides excellent structural strength, uniform load distribution and efficient material flow.

Common silo configurations include flat bottom silos, cone bottom silos and hopper bottom silos. The selection depends on material characteristics, discharge requirements and the required storage capacity.

What is a Bunker?

A bunker is a storage structure generally having a rectangular cross-section and a hopper bottom for controlled discharge of bulk materials. Bunkers are commonly used in power plants, steel plants and material handling systems for storing coal, limestone, ore and other coarse materials.

Unlike silos, bunkers are often integrated with feeders, conveyors and crushers where controlled material withdrawal is required. The hopper geometry plays a major role in preventing flow problems such as arching and ratholing.

Importance of Effective Storage Volume

The geometric volume of a silo or bunker does not represent the actual usable storage capacity. Bulk materials naturally form a slope due to gravity, known as the angle of repose. The space occupied by this material slope reduces the available storage volume.

Effective volume calculations are therefore important for determining actual operating capacity and avoiding insufficient storage during plant operation.

What is Angle of Repose?

The angle of repose is the maximum angle at which a pile of bulk material remains stable without sliding. It depends on particle size, moisture content, material density, surface roughness and cohesion between particles.

A higher angle of repose creates a steeper material cone and reduces the effective storage capacity. Engineers use this value while designing hopper geometry and estimating usable storage volume.

  • Cement: 30° – 35°
  • Fly Ash: 25° – 30°
  • Coal: 35° – 45°
  • Limestone: 35° – 40°
  • Sand: 30° – 40°

Types of Bulk Material Flow Patterns

The flow pattern inside a silo or bunker directly affects discharge reliability and material handling performance.

  • Mass Flow: All material moves during discharge. This provides uniform flow and is preferred for cohesive powders.
  • Funnel Flow: Material flows through a central channel while stagnant material remains near the walls. This can cause segregation and material ageing.
  • Ratholing: A stable empty channel forms above the outlet while material around it remains stationary.
  • Arching: Material forms a bridge above the outlet and prevents discharge.

Engineering Design Procedure

A typical silo and bunker sizing procedure followed by engineers includes:

  • Determine required storage quantity and operating hours.
  • Identify bulk material properties such as density and angle of repose.
  • Select suitable silo or bunker geometry.
  • Calculate gross geometric volume.
  • Estimate effective storage volume considering material slope.
  • Check hopper angle and outlet size for reliable discharge.
  • Perform structural design and load calculations.

Important Design Considerations

  • Hopper angle must be selected based on material flow characteristics.
  • Outlet size should prevent blockage and uncontrolled discharge.
  • Moisture content can significantly affect flow behaviour.
  • Bulk density variation affects actual stored quantity.
  • Structural loads from stored material must be considered.
  • Vibration and aeration systems may be required for difficult materials.
  • Bridging and ratholing should be evaluated during design.

Calculation Assumptions

  • Uniform bulk material distribution is assumed.
  • Material density remains constant.
  • Storage geometry is considered ideal.
  • No structural deformation is considered.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is effective storage volume lower than total volume?

Effective volume is lower because bulk materials form natural slopes due to the angle of repose and cannot occupy the complete geometric volume.

Why are silos generally circular?

Circular silos provide better structural strength, uniform stress distribution and efficient storage compared with rectangular structures.

Why are bunkers generally rectangular?

Rectangular bunkers are easier to integrate with feeders, conveyors and plant material handling equipment.

Can this calculator calculate material weight?

This calculator calculates volume. Material weight can be calculated separately by multiplying effective volume by the bulk density of the stored material.

Does moisture affect silo and bunker sizing?

Yes. Moisture changes material flow behaviour, bulk density and discharge performance, therefore it should be considered during detailed design.

Can this calculator replace detailed silo design?

No. This tool provides preliminary volume estimation. Final design requires structural analysis, flow testing and verification according to applicable engineering standards.
